## What Are Stablecoins?
When crypto prices swing 5 - 20% in a day, how do traders, builders, and everyday users keep a steady dollar value on-chain? That is the job of stablecoins. Think of them as digital dollars, euros, or gold that live on **[blockchain](/posts/what-is-blockchain)** rails, designed to hold a consistent price.

You will see them quoted as “pegged 1:1” to a reference asset like USD. The peg is not magic - it is enforced by collateral and redemption rules or, historically, by algorithms that tried to track price with supply changes. In this guide, you will learn how stablecoins work, why they matter, what can go wrong, and how to use them safely.

By the end, you should feel comfortable comparing different models, spotting risks like depegs, and choosing practical next steps for buying, storing, and transacting with them.

## Why Stablecoins Matter In Crypto And Beyond
Stablecoins are the quiet plumbing of digital finance. They simplify pricing, settlement, and savings for people who want the speed of crypto without constant price swings. Traders use them as a base currency to quote pairs and park profits. Developers use them as building blocks in **[DeFi](/posts/what-is-defi)** lending, payments, and derivatives. Everyday users rely on them for remittances and as a hedge against local currency inflation.

The appeal starts with stability. If you are familiar with **[Bitcoin](/posts/what-is-bitcoin)** or other volatile assets, you know market moves can be dramatic. Stablecoins reduce that exposure by anchoring to assets like USD or short-term Treasuries. This lets you budget, pay invoices, and settle trades without the constant mark-to-market stress highlighted in our overview of **[crypto volatility](/posts/crypto-volatility)**.

Finally, stablecoins operate 24/7 with near-instant settlement. International wires that take 2 - 5 business days can be replaced by minutes-long transfers at a fraction of the cost. That speed is not just convenient - it unlocks new business models where money moves as quickly as data.

## How Stablecoins Work: Pegs, Collateral, And Redemption
At a high level, a stablecoin maintains its target price through two pillars: credible collateral and a clear way to convert tokens back to the reference asset. If you can reliably redeem 1 token for $1, arbitrageurs will help keep the price tightly aligned with the peg. When price drifts to $0.99, buying and redeeming for $1 earns $0.01 per token. If it drifts to $1.01, selling brings it back down.

Issuers typically hold reserves such as cash, bank deposits, and short-duration U.S. Treasuries. These instruments aim for high liquidity and low risk so redemptions are honored even during market stress. Some decentralized models overcollateralize with crypto assets like ETH, locking more than $1 of value for every $1 of stablecoins issued to withstand price swings. A few experimental designs tried to maintain pegs algorithmically by changing supply - these have a checkered history and taught the market hard lessons in 2022.

Stablecoins run on multiple networks. Many first launched on **[Ethereum](/posts/what-is-ethereum)** and later expanded to faster, cheaper chains. Regardless of chain, the core mechanics are the same: mint on deposit of collateral, burn on redemption, and keep reserves transparent enough to preserve trust.

## Major Types Of Stablecoins
Most stablecoins fall into a few categories that map to how they try to keep the peg. Understanding these models will help you compare trade-offs in transparency, decentralization, and responsiveness during volatility.

- **Fiat-Backed (Custodial)**: Issued by companies that hold dollars or dollar-like assets. Redemption typically requires going through the issuer. Pros include tight pegs and broad adoption. Cons include counterparty and regulatory exposure.
- **Crypto-Backed (Overcollateralized)**: Minted against on-chain collateral like ETH or tokenized Treasuries, with loan-to-value ratios commonly at 50 - 80%. Pros include transparency and censorship resistance. Cons include liquidation risk during sharp drawdowns.
- **Algorithmic or Hybrid**: Attempt to hold the peg by expanding or contracting supply, sometimes with partial collateral. Pros include capital efficiency. Cons include reflexive risk during panics - several have failed when confidence broke.
- **Commodity-Backed**: Pegged to assets such as gold. Pros include diversification beyond fiat. Cons include storage, audit, and liquidity constraints.

Each type solves the peg problem differently. Custodial models lean on real-world assets and audits. Crypto-backed designs lean on overcollateralization and automated liquidation. Algorithmic systems rely on market incentives - which can unravel if users doubt future redemption.

## Risks, Depegs, And What 2022 Taught Us
Peg stability is earned, not assumed. The highest profile failure was the TerraUSD collapse in May 2022, when an algorithmic design lost its anchor and cascaded to near zero within days. The saga, associated with **[Do Kwon](/crypto-ogs/do-kwon)**, showed how self-referential collateral and circular incentives can unwind quickly when confidence evaporates. A related cautionary case involved **[Justin Sun](/crypto-ogs/justin-sun)**, whose Tron-based USDD briefly slipped below its dollar peg during the same 2022 turmoil, underscoring that even reserve-assisted algorithmic hybrids stay fragile when trust wavers.

Even fiat-backed coins can wobble. In March 2023, a portion of one issuer’s reserves was briefly stuck at a failed bank, and its coin traded down to roughly $0.88 before recovering as access to funds was clarified. The lesson was simple but critical: peg integrity depends on where the money is, how it is held, and whether it can be accessed fast during stress.

Risks cluster into a few buckets: reserve quality and liquidity, operational controls, regulatory posture, and chain-specific concerns like smart contract bugs or bridge failures. Good designs harden each layer - holding short-dated, high-quality assets, publishing timely attestations, segmenting risk across banks and custodians, and minimizing complex, brittle dependencies.

## How To Use And Acquire Stablecoins Safely
There are two common paths: buy on an exchange or mint on-chain against collateral. For most beginners, regulated exchanges are the simplest route. You can purchase popular stablecoins on platforms like **[Coinbase](/exchanges/coinbase)**, **[Binance](/exchanges/binance)**, and **[Kraken](/exchanges/kraken)**, or swap on decentralized exchanges such as **[Uniswap](/exchanges/uniswap)**. If you are new to trading venues, start with our primer on **[understanding crypto exchanges](/posts/understanding-crypto-exchanges)** to compare fees, liquidity, and custody options.

Once you hold stablecoins, decide where to store them based on how often you move funds. Long-term balances typically belong in a self-custody wallet with strong backups and hardware security. Hot wallets suit frequent, small transfers. If you are unsure how to evaluate options, review our guide on **[how to store your cryptocurrency](/posts/how-to-store-crypto)** for step-by-step setup and best practices.

Finally, be mindful of taxes. Swapping in and out of stablecoins can be taxable depending on your jurisdiction, as can staking or yield earnings derived from them. ## Regulatory Landscape In 2024
Policy is catching up to practice. In the European Union, the Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation - MiCA - began rolling out in 2024 with specific rules for asset-referenced and e-money tokens. These include reserve quality, redemption rights, and disclosure standards designed to protect users and reduce systemic risk. Several other jurisdictions have either proposed or enacted stablecoin-specific rules that focus on segregation of funds, liquidity, and supervision similar to e-money regimes.

In the United States, draft legislation has centered on reserve requirements and oversight, with state and federal roles still being debated as of late 2024. Regardless of region, the trend is clear: regulators expect high-quality, short-duration assets, audited reporting, and robust redemption processes. A freelance designer in Buenos Aires invoices a client in New York. Last year, she invoiced in local currency and lost 12% of value during a 3-week payment delay. This year, she uses a dollar stablecoin. The client pays within minutes, fees are under $5, and her budget remains intact while she decides when to convert to pesos.

A crypto market maker needs to rebalance inventory across three exchanges over the weekend. Bank wires will not settle until Monday. Using stablecoins, he clears transfers in under 10 minutes per venue, avoids overnight price risk, and captures an additional 0.15% spread opportunity that would have vanished with slower rails.

## Choosing The Right Stablecoin: A Simple Checklist
Picking a stablecoin is like choosing a savings account and a payment network rolled into one. Focus on transparency, redemption, and operational strength before you chase yield or convenience.

- **Reserves And Reporting**: Are assets cash and short-term Treasuries, with frequent attestations by recognized firms? Look for clarity on bank partners and custody.
- **Redemption Mechanics**: Who can redeem and how fast? Direct redemption for qualified users is a positive signal, as is consistent secondary market liquidity.
- **On-Chain Design**: For crypto-backed models, is collateral over 100%, with conservative liquidation parameters and circuit breakers for stress scenarios?
- **Chain And Contract Risk**: Has the contract been audited, and does the token rely on bridges or wrapped assets that add complexity?
- **Regulatory Posture**: Does the issuer operate under clear rules where it is domiciled, and have they navigated prior market events smoothly?

As you evaluate, remember that small differences in reserve duration, banking partners, or oracle design can matter greatly in a crunch. Read the fine print.

## Stablecoins, CBDCs, And Bank Deposits - What Is The Difference?
Stablecoins are privately issued tokens that aim to track a currency. Bank deposits are liabilities of a specific bank with deposit insurance up to certain limits, moving on legacy rails. CBDCs would be liabilities of a central bank, potentially programmable but subject to national policy choices. The parallels can be helpful, but the practical distinctions are critical for risk analysis and privacy expectations. ## Common Myths And Misconceptions
“Stablecoins never move off $1.” Even the best ones can trade at a small premium or discount during stress. The real test is how quickly and predictably the price snaps back. “Fully backed” does not mean “zero risk” - pay attention to where and how reserves are held. “Decentralized means safer.” Sometimes yes, sometimes not. On-chain transparency is powerful, but overcollateralized designs face liquidation cascades if oracles or markets fail under pressure.

Finally, “more yield is always better.” Extra yield often means extra duration risk, lending risk, or hidden leverage. If a coin pays surprisingly high returns, ask which risks you are underwriting.
